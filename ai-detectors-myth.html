How AI Detectors Work: Myths and Reality

Published: May 2026 · Reading time: 6 min · Author: HumanType Team

The rise of AI detection

As AI-generated text floods the internet, universities, publishers, and platforms have deployed AI detection tools. These tools claim to identify whether a piece of text was written by a human or generated by a language model. But how accurate are they, really? And what signals do they actually use?

Myth #1: AI detectors are 99% accurate

Reality check: even the best AI detectors have significant error rates. Studies show that commercial AI detectors have false positive rates ranging from 5% to 15%, meaning they incorrectly flag human-written text as AI-generated. This is especially dangerous in academic settings, where false accusations can have serious consequences.

Detectors work on probabilities, not certainties. A 95% confidence score doesn't mean the text is 95% AI — it means the model is 95% confident in its prediction, which is a very different thing.

Myth #2: AI detectors understand meaning

AI detectors don't "understand" text the way humans do. They analyze statistical patterns: how predictable each word is given the previous words (perplexity), how much sentence structure varies (burstiness), and what typographic symbols are present. A text with low perplexity — meaning it's highly predictable — is more likely to be flagged as AI-generated, regardless of its actual content quality.

Myth #3: Typographic symbols don't matter

This is one of the most overlooked factors. AI detectors don't just look at words — they look at the actual Unicode characters. An em dash (—) is a different code point from a hyphen (-). Curly quotes (“ ”) differ from straight quotes ("). These differences create detectable patterns because most humans don't use these symbols in casual writing.

In one informal test, simply replacing typographic symbols in an AI-generated essay reduced the detection score by over 30%. When combined with other humanization techniques, the text became indistinguishable from human writing.

What detectors actually measure

  • Perplexity: How "surprised" a language model would be by each word. Human writing tends to have higher perplexity (more variation), while AI text is more predictable.
  • Burstiness: The variation in sentence length and complexity. Humans tend to mix short and long sentences; AI often produces uniform sentence structures.
  • Typographic fingerprints: The presence of specific Unicode characters strongly associated with AI training data.
  • Statistical oddities: Unusual word frequency distributions, rare token usage, and other mathematical patterns.

The bottom line

AI detectors are useful screening tools but not infallible judges. Reducing detection scores requires a multi-layered approach: normalizing typography, varying sentence structure, adding minor imperfections, and injecting genuine human voice.
